Feedback for Real-Walking VR System

ETH Zurich Fall 2015

For my semester project at ETH Zurich, I worked on feedback mechanisms for real walking virtual reality systems under the advisorship of Professor Andreas Kunz and Markus Zank. Real-walking virtual reality is where your movements in a digital environment are enabled by real, physical walking. A upwards facing camera was attached to an Oculus Rift headset to pick up landmarks on the ceiling in a large room. Based upon which visual landmarks were seen in the camera's frame, the user's headset could be localized.

When exploring a virtual world while walking in a digital world, some issues exist. The first is that you can run into a physical wall. The virtual world might seem open and clear, but if an obstacle exists in the physical world, the user can run into it. Redirection algorithms were developed that allow a user to be steered away from physical obstacles unknowingly, by subtly changing the view frame of the virtual reality headset to induce a user to walk in arcs instead of in straight lines.

Another problem that exists is that users can walk through digital walls, thus ignoring any virtual maps or objectives that might exist. This can cause scenes to become very difficult to render because it is difficult to control a user's movement. I developed feedback mechanisms and algorithms to make deincentivized, unpleasant, or even impossible for users to walk through digital walls. I created a user study to test out these mechanisms.
